## 一、引言

The convergence of sequences and series of functions is a fundamental topic in mathematical analysis. While the notion of pointwise convergence is natural and easy to define, it often fails to preserve the properties we care about. For instance, a sequence of continuous functions may converge pointwise to a discontinuous function, and term-by-term integration of a series may not yield the correct result.

Uniform convergence provides a stronger mode of convergence that addresses these deficiencies. When a sequence of functions converges uniformly, many desirable properties—continuity, integrability, and differentiability under appropriate conditions—are preserved in the limit.

In this paper, we develop the theory of uniform convergence from first principles, prove the key theorems, and explore applications. We pay particular attention to the Weierstrass M-test, a practical criterion for establishing uniform convergence of series. Along the way, we construct examples that illustrate the necessity of our hypotheses and demonstrate the power of the theory.

## 二、Pointwise vs. Uniform Convergence

### 2.1 Definitions

**Definition 2.1.** Let $\{f_n\}$ be a sequence of functions defined on a set $E$. We say that $\{f_n\}$ *converges pointwise* to $f$ on $E$ if for every $x \in E$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N = N(x, \varepsilon)$ such that:
$$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon \quad \text{for all } n \geq N.$$

**Definition 2.2.** The sequence $\{f_n\}$ *converges uniformly* to $f$ on $E$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N = N(\varepsilon)$ such that:
$$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon \quad \text{for all } x \in E \text{ and all } n \geq N.$$

The crucial difference is that in uniform convergence, $N$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on $x$.

### 2.2 A First Example

**Example 2.3.** Consider $f_n(x) = x^n$ on $[0, 1]$. 

*Pointwise limit:* For $x \in [0, 1)$, $f_n(x) \to 0$. For $x = 1$, $f_n(1) = 1$ for all $n$. So:
$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & 0 \leq x < 1 \\ 1 & x = 1 \end{cases}$$

The limit function is discontinuous at $x = 1$, despite each $f_n$ being continuous.

*Uniform convergence?* No. For any $n$, choose $x = (1/2)^{1/n}$. Then $f_n(x) = 1/2$, but $f(x) = 0$. Thus $|f_n(x) - f(x)| = 1/2$, so we cannot make this uniformly small.

**Geometric interpretation:** Uniform convergence means that for large $n$, the entire graph of $f_n$ lies within an $\varepsilon$-tube around the graph of $f$. Pointwise convergence only requires this at each individual point.

## 三、Properties Preserved by Uniform Convergence

### 3.1 Continuity

**Theorem 3.1.** If $\{f_n\}$ converges uniformly to $f$ on $E$, and each $f_n$ is continuous at $x_0 \in E$, then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$.

*Proof.* Let $\varepsilon > 0$. By uniform convergence, there exists $N$ such that for all $n \geq N$ and all $x \in E$:
$$|f_n(x) - f(x)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{3}.$$

Since $f_N$ is continuous at $x_0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that:
$$|f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| < \frac{\varepsilon}{3} \quad \text{whenever } |x - x_0| < \delta.$$

Then for $|x - x_0| < \delta$:

\begin{align*}

|f(x) - f(x_0)| &\leq |f(x) - f_N(x)| + |f_N(x) - f_N(x_0)| + |f_N(x_0) - f(x_0)| \\

&< \frac{\varepsilon}{3} + \frac{\varepsilon}{3} + \frac{\varepsilon}{3} = \varepsilon.
\end{align*}

Thus $f$ is continuous at $x_0$. ∎

**Remark.** Example 2.3 shows that pointwise convergence is not sufficient for this result.

### 3.2 Integration

**Theorem 3.2.** If $\{f_n\}$ converges uniformly to $f$ on $[a, b]$, and each $f_n$ is Riemann integrable, then $f$ is Riemann integrable and:
$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \int_a^b f_n(x)\, dx = \int_a^b f(x)\, dx.$$

*Proof sketch.* One shows that $f$ is integrable by verifying the Riemann criterion using the uniform approximation. The integral formula follows from:
$$\left|\int_a^b f_n - \int_a^b f\right| \leq \int_a^b |f_n - f| \leq (b-a)\|f_n - f\|_\infty \to 0.$$

### 3.3 Differentiation

Uniform convergence alone does not preserve differentiability. We need additional hypotheses:

**Theorem 3.3.** Suppose $\{f_n\}$ converges pointwise to $f$ on $[a, b]$, each $f_n$ is continuously differentiable, and $\{f_n'\}$ converges uniformly on $[a, b]$. Then $f$ is differentiable and:
$$f'(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} f_n'(x).$$

## 四、The Weierstrass M-Test

### 4.1 Statement and Proof

**Theorem 4.1 (Weierstrass M-Test).** Let $\{f_n\}$ be a sequence of functions on $E$. Suppose there exist constants $M_n$ such that:
$$|f_n(x)| \leq M_n \quad \text{for all } x \in E,$$

and $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} M_n$ converges. Then $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n$ converges uniformly on $E$.

*Proof.* By the comparison test, $\sum f_n(x)$ converges absolutely for each $x$. Let $f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x)$.

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, since $\sum M_n$ converges, there exists $N$ such that:
$$\sum_{n=N}^{\infty} M_n < \varepsilon.$$

Then for all $x \in E$:
$$\left|f(x) - \sum_{n=1}^{N-1} f_n(x)\right| = \left|\sum_{n=N}^{\infty} f_n(x)\right| \leq \sum_{n=N}^{\infty} |f_n(x)| \leq \sum_{n=N}^{\infty} M_n < \varepsilon.$$

This shows uniform convergence. ∎

### 4.2 Applications

**Example 4.2.** The series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}$ converges uniformly on $\mathbb{R}$.

*Proof.* Take $M_n = 1/n^2$. Since $\sum 1/n^2$ converges and $|\sin(nx)/n^2| \leq 1/n^2$, the M-test applies. ∎

**Corollary 4.3.** The sum $f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$.

## 五、A Nowhere Differentiable Function

### 5.1 The Construction

One of the most striking applications of uniform convergence is the construction of continuous functions that are nowhere differentiable.

**Theorem 5.1 (Weierstrass).** There exists a function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ that is continuous everywhere but differentiable nowhere.

*Proof.* Define:
$$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)$$
where $0 < a < 1$, $b$ is an odd integer, and $ab > 1 + \frac{3\pi}{2}$.

*Continuity:* Taking $M_n = a^n$, since $\sum a^n$ converges (geometric series), the M-test shows uniform convergence on $\mathbb{R}$. Since each term is continuous, $f$ is continuous.

*Nowhere differentiability:* The proof that $f$ is nowhere differentiable involves careful estimation of difference quotients. The key observation is that the high-frequency terms oscillate too rapidly for the function to have a well-defined derivative at any point. ∎

### 5.2 Significance

This construction challenged the prevailing intuition of the 19th century, which held that continuous functions must be differentiable "most of the time." It demonstrates the importance of rigorous definitions and careful proof in analysis.

## 六、Conclusion

We have explored the theory of uniform convergence, beginning with its definition and distinction from pointwise convergence. We proved that uniform convergence preserves continuity and integrability, and established the Weierstrass M-test as a practical tool for proving uniform convergence.

The construction of a continuous nowhere differentiable function illustrates the power of these techniques and their role in shaping modern analysis. Uniform convergence remains a fundamental concept, underlying many advanced topics in real and complex analysis, functional analysis, and the theory of differential equations.
