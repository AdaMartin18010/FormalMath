## 一、引言

The problem of solving polynomial equations has fascinated mathematicians for millennia. The quadratic formula was known to ancient Babylonians, and solutions for cubic and quartic equations were discovered during the Italian Renaissance. However, the question of whether a general quintic equation could be solved by radicals remained open until the early 19th century.

In 1824, Niels Henrik Abel proved that the general quintic equation is not solvable by radicals. Later, Évariste Galois developed a general theory—now known as Galois theory—that provides a complete characterization of when polynomial equations are solvable by radicals. The key insight is that the solvability of a polynomial is determined by the structure of its Galois group.

In this paper, we present a complete proof of the insolvability of the general quintic. We assume familiarity with basic group theory and field extensions, but develop all necessary Galois theory from first principles. Our goal is to provide a clear, conceptual understanding of this landmark result.

This paper is organized as follows. In Section 2, we review the necessary background on field extensions and automorphisms. Section 3 introduces Galois groups and the fundamental theorem of Galois theory. Section 4 develops the criterion for solvability by radicals. In Section 5, we prove that $S_5$ is not solvable and deduce the insolvability of the quintic. Finally, Section 6 provides concrete examples and discusses historical context.

## 二、Field Extensions and Automorphisms

### 2.1 Basic Definitions

We begin by reviewing the fundamental concepts of field extensions.

**Definition 2.1.** A *field extension* $E/F$ consists of a field $F$ contained in a larger field $E$. The *degree* of the extension, denoted $[E:F]$, is the dimension of $E$ as a vector space over $F$.

**Example 2.2.** The extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q}$ has degree 2, with basis $\{1, \sqrt{2}\}$.

**Definition 2.3.** An element $\alpha \in E$ is *algebraic* over $F$ if there exists a nonzero polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ such that $f(\alpha) = 0$. The extension $E/F$ is *algebraic* if every element of $E$ is algebraic over $F$.

### 2.2 Splitting Fields

**Definition 2.4.** Let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be a polynomial. A *splitting field* for $f$ over $F$ is an extension $E/F$ such that:
1. $f$ splits completely into linear factors in $E[x]$;
2. $E$ is generated by $F$ and the roots of $f$.

**Theorem 2.5.** Every polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ has a splitting field, and any two splitting fields are isomorphic.

*Proof sketch.* The existence follows by repeatedly adjoining roots. The uniqueness follows from the isomorphism extension theorem. ∎

### 2.3 Automorphisms

**Definition 2.6.** An *automorphism* of a field extension $E/F$ is a field isomorphism $\sigma: E \to E$ that fixes $F$ pointwise, i.e., $\sigma(a) = a$ for all $a \in F$.

The set of all automorphisms of $E/F$ forms a group under composition, denoted $\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)$.

**Example 2.7.** For the extension $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2})/\mathbb{Q}$, the automorphism group has order 2, generated by $\sigma$ where $\sigma(\sqrt{2}) = -\sqrt{2}$.

## 三、Galois Theory Fundamentals

### 3.1 Galois Extensions

**Definition 3.1.** A finite extension $E/F$ is *Galois* if it is both normal and separable. Equivalently, $|\operatorname{Aut}(E/F)| = [E:F]$.

For a Galois extension, we write $\operatorname{Gal}(E/F) := \operatorname{Aut}(E/F)$ and call it the *Galois group* of $E/F$.

**Theorem 3.2 (Fundamental Theorem of Galois Theory).** Let $E/F$ be a finite Galois extension with Galois group $G = \operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$. Then:

1. There is a bijective correspondence between intermediate fields $F \subseteq K \subseteq E$ and subgroups $H \subseteq G$, given by:
   $$K \mapsto \operatorname{Gal}(E/K) \quad \text{and} \quad H \mapsto E^H$$
   where $E^H$ is the fixed field of $H$.

2. The correspondence is inclusion-reversing.

3. $[E:K] = |\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)|$ and $[K:F] = [G : \operatorname{Gal}(E/K)]$.

4. $K/F$ is Galois if and only if $\operatorname{Gal}(E/K) \trianglelefteq G$, in which case $\operatorname{Gal}(K/F) \cong G/\operatorname{Gal}(E/K)$.

### 3.2 The Galois Group of a Polynomial

**Definition 3.3.** Let $f(x) \in F[x]$ be a polynomial with splitting field $E$. The *Galois group of $f$* is $\operatorname{Gal}(f) := \operatorname{Gal}(E/F)$.

**Proposition 3.4.** If $f \in F[x]$ is separable of degree $n$, then $\operatorname{Gal}(f)$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_n$.

*Proof.* The Galois group permutes the $n$ distinct roots of $f$, giving an embedding into $S_n$. ∎

## 四、Solvability by Radicals

### 4.1 Radical Extensions

**Definition 4.1.** An extension $E/F$ is a *radical extension* if there exists a tower of fields:
$$F = F_0 \subseteq F_1 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq F_m = E$$
where $F_{i+1} = F_i(\alpha_i)$ with $\alpha_i^{n_i} \in F_i$ for some integer $n_i \geq 1$.

A polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ is *solvable by radicals* if its splitting field is contained in a radical extension of $F$.

### 4.2 The Solvability Criterion

**Definition 4.2.** A group $G$ is *solvable* if there exists a chain of subgroups:
$$1 = G_0 \trianglelefteq G_1 \trianglelefteq \cdots \trianglelefteq G_m = G$$
such that each quotient $G_{i+1}/G_i$ is abelian.

**Theorem 4.3 (Galois' Solvability Criterion).** A separable polynomial $f(x) \in F[x]$ is solvable by radicals if and only if $\operatorname{Gal}(f)$ is a solvable group.

*Proof sketch.* (⇒) If $f$ is solvable by radicals, the Galois group inherits the solvability structure from the radical tower.

(⇐) If $\operatorname{Gal}(f)$ is solvable, we can construct a radical extension containing the splitting field using the theory of roots of unity and Kummer theory. ∎

## 五、The Insolvability of the Quintic

### 5.1 The Symmetric Group $S_5$

**Theorem 5.1.** The symmetric group $S_5$ is not solvable.

*Proof.* We show that $A_5$ is simple and non-abelian, which implies $S_5$ is not solvable.

First, $A_5$ is simple (a standard result in group theory). Since $|A_5| = 60 > 1$ and $A_5$ is non-abelian, any composition series for $S_5$ must include $A_5$ as a factor. But $A_5$ is simple and non-abelian, so it cannot have a composition series with abelian factors.

Therefore, $S_5$ is not solvable. ∎

### 5.2 The General Quintic

**Theorem 5.2 (Abel-Ruffini).** The general polynomial equation of degree 5:
$$x^5 + a_4x^4 + a_3x^3 + a_2x^2 + a_1x + a_0 = 0$$
is not solvable by radicals over $\mathbb{Q}(a_0, \ldots, a_4)$.

*Proof.* The general quintic has Galois group $S_5$ over its coefficient field. Since $S_5$ is not solvable (Theorem 5.1), by the Galois solvability criterion (Theorem 4.3), the general quintic is not solvable by radicals. ∎

### 5.3 Specific Examples

**Corollary 5.4.** The polynomial $f(x) = x^5 - x + 1 \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$ is not solvable by radicals.

*Proof.* One can verify that $\operatorname{Gal}(f) \cong S_5$. Since $S_5$ is not solvable, $f$ is not solvable by radicals. ∎

## 六、Discussion and Conclusion

### 6.1 Historical Context

The insolvability of the quintic represents a turning point in algebra. Prior to Abel and Galois, the dominant approach was to search for increasingly complex formulas. The realization that no such formula exists fundamentally changed the nature of algebraic inquiry.

Galois' theory also laid the groundwork for modern abstract algebra, demonstrating the power of studying algebraic structures through their symmetries.

### 6.2 Modern Developments

While the general quintic is not solvable by radicals, there are other approaches:

1. **Bring radicals**: The general quintic can be solved using special functions (Bring radicals).

2. **Numerical methods**: Practical approximations can be obtained efficiently using numerical techniques.

3. **Algebraic geometry**: The theory of elliptic curves and modular forms provides alternative perspectives on polynomial equations.

### 6.3 Conclusion

We have presented a complete proof of the insolvability of the general quintic by radicals. The key steps were:

1. Developing the fundamentals of Galois theory
2. Establishing the solvability criterion in terms of the Galois group
3. Proving that $S_5$ is not solvable
4. Concluding that the general quintic is not solvable by radicals

This result not only resolves a classical problem but also illustrates the profound connection between field theory and group theory that lies at the heart of modern algebra.
