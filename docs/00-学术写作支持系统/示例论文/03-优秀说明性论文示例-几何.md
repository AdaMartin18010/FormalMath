## 摘要

> The Gauss-Bonnet theorem stands as one of the most profound results in differential geometry, connecting local geometric properties (curvature) to global topological invariants (Euler characteristic). We present a self-contained proof of this theorem for compact surfaces, beginning with the fundamental concepts of Gaussian curvature and geodesic curvature. Our approach emphasizes the intuitive geometric content while maintaining mathematical rigor, and we illustrate the theory with concrete examples including the sphere, torus, and surfaces of higher genus.

---

## 一、引言

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem represents a pinnacle of classical differential geometry. In its simplest form, it states that for a compact surface $M$ without boundary:
$$\int_M K\, dA = 2\pi \chi(M)$$
where $K$ is the Gaussian curvature and $\chi(M)$ is the Euler characteristic.

This remarkable formula bridges two seemingly distinct worlds: the *local* world of differential geometry, where we study curvature through calculus, and the *global* world of topology, where we classify surfaces up to continuous deformation. The fact that the integral of curvature—a quantity defined infinitesimally—depends only on the topology of the surface is genuinely surprising.

In this paper, we develop the necessary machinery to prove the Gauss-Bonnet theorem and explore its implications. Our treatment assumes familiarity with multivariable calculus and basic surface theory, but we develop all specialized concepts from first principles.

## 二、Curvature of Surfaces

### 2.1 Surfaces in $\mathbb{R}^3$

A *regular surface* $S \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ is a 2-dimensional submanifold. Locally, surfaces can be parametrized by maps $\mathbf{x}: U \to S$ where $U \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is open and the differential $d\mathbf{x}$ has rank 2 everywhere.

**Example 2.1 (The Sphere).** The unit sphere $S^2$ can be parametrized (away from the poles) by:
$$\mathbf{x}(\theta, \varphi) = (\sin\theta\cos\varphi, \sin\theta\sin\varphi, \cos\theta).$$

### 2.2 The First and Second Fundamental Forms

At each point $p \in S$, the tangent plane $T_pS$ is a 2-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$. The *first fundamental form* $I$ is simply the restriction of the Euclidean inner product to $T_pS$.

The *second fundamental form* $II$ measures how the surface bends away from its tangent plane. It is defined using the *shape operator* (or Weingarten map) $S: T_pS \to T_pS$.

**Definition 2.2.** The *principal curvatures* $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$ at $p$ are the eigenvalues of the shape operator.

### 2.3 Gaussian Curvature

**Definition 2.3.** The *Gaussian curvature* at $p$ is:
$$K = \kappa_1 \kappa_2 = \det(S).$$

**Example 2.4.** 
- On the unit sphere, $K = 1$ everywhere.
- On a plane, $K = 0$ everywhere.
- On a saddle surface, $K < 0$.

The sign of $K$ has important geometric meaning: $K > 0$ means the surface is locally convex (like a sphere), $K < 0$ means it is locally saddle-shaped, and $K = 0$ means it is locally flat in at least one direction.

## 三、Geodesic Curvature

### 3.1 Geodesics

A *geodesic* is a curve that locally minimizes distance. Equivalently, it is a curve with "zero acceleration" in the surface.

**Definition 3.1.** A unit-speed curve $\gamma$ in $S$ is a *geodesic* if its geodesic curvature $\kappa_g = 0$.

### 3.2 Geodesic Curvature of a Curve

For a general curve $\gamma$ in $S$, we can decompose its curvature vector into normal and tangential components:
$$\kappa \mathbf{n} = \kappa_n \mathbf{N} + \kappa_g (\mathbf{N} \times \gamma')$$
where $\mathbf{N}$ is the surface normal.

**Definition 3.2.** The *geodesic curvature* $\kappa_g$ measures how much $\gamma$ deviates from being a geodesic.

**Example 3.3.** 
- Great circles on $S^2$ are geodesics, so $\kappa_g = 0$.
- A small circle of latitude has constant nonzero geodesic curvature.

## 四、The Local Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

### 4.1 Geodesic Triangles

A *geodesic triangle* is a region bounded by three geodesic segments.

**Theorem 4.1 (Local Gauss-Bonnet).** Let $T$ be a geodesic triangle on a surface $S$ with interior angles $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$. Then:
$$\int_T K\, dA = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi.$$

*Proof sketch.* The proof involves integrating the geodesic curvature around the boundary and applying Stokes' theorem. The key formula is:
$$\int_T K\, dA + \int_{\partial T} \kappa_g\, ds = 2\pi - (\pi - \alpha) - (\pi - \beta) - (\pi - \gamma).$$

For a geodesic triangle, $\kappa_g = 0$ on the boundary, giving the result. ∎

**Geometric interpretation:** The quantity $\alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi$ (the "angle excess") measures how much the triangle deviates from Euclidean geometry. On the sphere ($K > 0$), triangles have angle excess; on hyperbolic surfaces ($K < 0$), they have angle defect.

### 4.2 Example: Spherical Triangles

On the unit sphere, $K = 1$, so:
$$\text{Area}(T) = \alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi.$$

For example, a triangle formed by the equator and two meridians separated by angle $\theta$ has:
$$\alpha = \beta = \frac{\pi}{2}, \quad \gamma = \theta, \quad \text{Area} = \theta.$$

## 五、The Global Gauss-Bonnet Theorem

### 5.1 Triangulations and the Euler Characteristic

A *triangulation* of a surface $M$ is a decomposition into triangles meeting edge-to-edge.

**Definition 5.1.** The *Euler characteristic* is:
$$\chi(M) = V - E + F$$
where $V$, $E$, $F$ are the numbers of vertices, edges, and faces in any triangulation.

**Theorem 5.2.** $\chi(M)$ is independent of the triangulation and depends only on the topology of $M$.

**Example 5.3.**
- Sphere: $\chi(S^2) = 2$
- Torus: $\chi(T^2) = 0$
- Genus $g$ surface: $\chi = 2 - 2g$

### 5.2 The Global Theorem

**Theorem 5.4 (Global Gauss-Bonnet).** Let $M$ be a compact surface without boundary. Then:
$$\int_M K\, dA = 2\pi \chi(M).$$

*Proof.* Triangulate $M$ with geodesic triangles $T_1, \ldots, T_F$. Applying the local Gauss-Bonnet theorem to each:
$$\int_{T_i} K\, dA = \alpha_i + \beta_i + \gamma_i - \pi.$$

Summing over all triangles:
$$\int_M K\, dA = \sum_{i=1}^F (\alpha_i + \beta_i + \gamma_i) - F\pi.$$

The sum of all angles equals $2\pi V$ (each vertex contributes $2\pi$). Also $3F = 2E$ (each face has 3 edges, each edge belongs to 2 faces). Thus:
$$\int_M K\, dA = 2\pi V - F\pi = 2\pi V - \pi(2E - 2F) = 2\pi(V - E + F) = 2\pi \chi(M).$$ ∎

## 六、Applications and Implications

### 6.1 Theorem of the Turning Tangent

**Corollary 6.1.** For a simple closed curve $\gamma$ on a surface:
$$\int_\gamma \kappa_g\, ds = 2\pi - \int_{\text{interior}} K\, dA.$$

On the plane ($K = 0$), this reduces to the classical theorem: the total curvature of a simple closed curve is $2\pi$.

### 6.2 The Hairball Theorem

**Corollary 6.2.** Every continuous vector field on $S^2$ has at least one zero.

*Proof.* If a nowhere-zero vector field existed, we could define a Gauss map with degree 0. But the Gauss-Bonnet theorem implies the degree must be $\chi(S^2)/2 = 1$. ∎

### 6.3 Uniformization

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem places constraints on the metrics a surface can support. For example, a genus $g \geq 2$ surface cannot have a metric with $K > 0$ everywhere, since $\chi < 0$.

---

## 七、Conclusion

The Gauss-Bonnet theorem exemplifies the deep connections between local and global geometry. By integrating curvature, we obtain a topological invariant—the Euler characteristic—that is independent of the specific geometric structure.

This theorem has far-reaching generalizations in modern geometry, including:
- The Chern-Gauss-Bonnet theorem for higher-dimensional manifolds
- The Atiyah-Singer index theorem
- String theory and quantum gravity

Understanding the classical Gauss-Bonnet theorem provides essential intuition for these advanced topics.
